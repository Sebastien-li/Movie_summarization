Profile of Die Hard

Die Hard can be described as rough, exciting, and clever. The plot revolves around a one-man army, a master criminal, and a tough law enforcer. The main genres are action and thriller. In terms of style, Die Hard has a Hollywood tone. In approach, it is serious and realistic. It is set, at least in part, in a skyscraper. Die Hard is located in Los Angeles. It takes place in the 1980s. It is based on a book. Die Hard has received attention for being a modern classic, essential viewing, and a blockbuster. It is well suited for a boys' night. Note that it involves profanity and violent content.

Summary

One of the greatest action movies of the late 1980s, DIE HARD ushered in a new standard for the genre.... more
One of the greatest action movies of the late 1980s, DIE HARD ushered in a new standard for the genre. With the dissolution of the Cold War, both the stereotypical Russian threat (represented in movies such as TOP GUN and RED DAWN) and the destructive egoist (as seen in OCTOPUSSY) became less fearful. With DIE HARD, director John McTiernan introduced Hollywood to a new type of villain: the terrorist entrepreneur. Alan Rickman stars as Hans Gruber, a relentless businessman whose lethal tactics achieve his goals. Unlike most '80s film villains who committed globally dangerous acts for liberty, genocide, or megalomania, DIE HARD's Gruber uses guns, explosives, and cunning to storm the Takagi Corporation's Christmas party and heist millions of dollars from the company. In addition, DIE HARD also saw the development of the clumsy or bad luck hero with John McClane (Bruce Willis), a man in the wrong place at the wrong time who chooses to intervene. Action fans would see this misfit hero archetype again in UNDER SIEGE (Steven Segal) and THE PHANTOM MENACE (Jar Jar Binks). Although DIE HARD contains many action movie cliches (one-liners, pyrotechnics), it also broke new ground in its genre. less

This might be the most exciting and compelling action motion picture ever caught on film. If there was a role that Bruce Willis was born to play, this is it. His believability is the difference between calling this a good action flick and THE perfect action movie. The special effects are dazzling. The comedic yet sincere one-liners carve a niche in your mind and remain forever. Alan Rickman is at his very best, and the chemistry between hero and villain is simply icing on the cake for this truly remarkable film.

Director John McTiernan, screenwriters Jeb Stuart and Steven deSouza, novelist Roderick Thorp, and actor Bruce Willis brought something entirely new to the screen in 1988: the flawed action hero. Sure, previous heroes had some flaw, like a girlfriend problem or being too vengeful (hello Charles Bronson) but Die Hard's John McClane was something else entirely: he's a bad husband, a bad father, a bad cop, probably has a drinking problem, smokes too much and wants to quit but can't. He swears too much. He makes bad decisions. Welcome to the action hero you can identify with! It's not too often a film can break formula and write a new one, but Die Hard did. Aside from McClane, Die Hard is filmed brilliantly. Utilizing multiple kinds of lenses, filters and focuses, the look of the film is wholly unique, often filmed in a fashion that heightens our sense of claustrophobia (increasing the relatability of McClane and the situation he is in). Some of the lenses squeeze the picture so much, in some shots Bruce Willis' head looks like it's 4 feet long. And it's influence continues today, all the way to television.
